The Quiet Cost of Convenience

dry

There is a particular kind of progress that arrives without argument. No one votes for it; no one is asked. It simply becomes the default, and within a season the alternative feels like an inconvenience you are choosing on purpose. The self-checkout. The algorithmic feed. The app that replaced the phone call that replaced the visit.

Each of these is, in isolation, a small mercy. Together they form a quiet politics — a slow renegotiation of what we owe each other, conducted entirely through interface design. The question is never put to us plainly, because to put it plainly would be to invite refusal.

> Convenience is the most underrated political force of our time. It does not > persuade; it erodes. It wins not by changing minds but by changing defaults.

What gets lost is rarely dramatic. It is the friction that used to carry meaning: the cashier who knew your mother, the wrong turn that became the good story, the boredom that used to precede an idea. We have optimized these away as inefficiencies, and we are only beginning to notice that some inefficiencies were load-bearing.

None of this is an argument against the new tools. It is an argument for noticing — for treating the default as a claim that can be examined rather than a fact that must be accepted. The most radical act available to most of us is simply to ask, before we tap accept: who designed this convenience, and what did they assume I would be willing to give up for it?

A society can lose something important without anyone deciding to discard it. It can simply stop choosing it, one frictionless default at a time. The remedy is not nostalgia. It is attention.
